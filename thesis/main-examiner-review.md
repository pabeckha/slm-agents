# Examiner Review — Agents with Small Language Models: Optimization Techniques for Reliable Reasoning and Tool Calling

**Author:** Paulo Ricardo Beckhauser de Araujo · **Reviewed:** 2026-06-09 · **Pages:** 60 (PDF), ~51 printed
**Reviewer note:** AI-assisted examiner review — to be verified and owned by the examiner.

## 1. Summary of the thesis

The thesis asks which optimization techniques make a small language model (Qwen 2.5, 0.5B–7B) reliable enough at tool calling to serve as the local tier of a cascade architecture. Three research questions are stated (p. 3): RQ1, the performance gap between unoptimized SLMs and frontier models on tool-calling benchmarks; RQ2, the marginal contribution of each technique (constrained decoding, prompt engineering, RAG, quantization, LoRA) applied cumulatively; RQ3, whether a fully optimized SLM configuration achieves production-viable tool-calling accuracy on consumer hardware. An incremental ablation across eleven configurations (plus three no-guidance controls) on BFCL v4 simple python, extended BFCL categories, and τ-bench retail concludes that the unoptimized gap is a format-compliance gap, that constrained decoding alone lifts the 7B model from 1.5% to 72.75%, that format-aligned LoRA reaches 76.75%, that prompting and RAG interventions reduce accuracy, and that single-call gains do not transfer to multi-turn tasks (4.35% on τ-bench).

## 2. Overall assessment

This is a well-designed, well-executed empirical thesis with an unusually honest treatment of negative results. The ablation ladder with isolation controls is methodologically clean, the format-vs-semantic failure taxonomy is a genuine organizing insight, and the misaligned-vs-aligned LoRA experiment (identical hyperparameters and near-identical training loss, 7.0 pp accuracy difference, pp. 26, 40) is the kind of controlled comparison most Master's theses never attempt. The threats-to-validity section (p. 38) acknowledges most of the right things.

What holds the work back is a set of claim-evidence gaps at the joints. The most serious: the 1.5% baseline is an artifact of the author's own minimal pipeline rather than a property of the model — Qwen 2.5 natively supports tool calling via its chat template, and the official BFCL harness evaluates it far above 1.5%, which the thesis never confronts. Second, the discussion's explanation of the parallel-category failure ("the two-stage pipeline is architecturally constrained to emit one function name and one argument object per inference call," p. 37) directly contradicts Section 4.8.3, where the same pipeline produces 1–4 calls per response on parallel multiple. Third, Figure 4.3's percentages do not reconcile with their stated denominator. None of these is fatal — all are fixable before the July submission — but as the document stands, they are exactly the questions a censor would open with.

**Indicative grade band (written thesis only, advisory): 7 (C), with a clear path to 10 (B).** The experimental work and honesty would support a 10; the unresolved baseline-fairness framing in a core claim (RQ1), the internal contradiction on the parallel category, and the figure/statistics issues are collectively "a number of weaknesses" in the 7-band sense. Resolving Issues 1–3 below and adding paired significance tests would move it to 10; additionally re-anchoring RQ1 against the official BFCL Qwen baseline and tightening the abstract's claims could put 12 in reach, since the underlying contribution survives that reframing.

## 3. Strengths

- **Clean incremental design with proper controls.** The eleven-configuration ladder (Fig. 3.1, p. 14) plus the no-guidance isolation variants (PE-ng, ITC-ng, RAG-ng, Section 4.2.5) lets the thesis attribute marginal effects credibly. Table 4.4 (CD's marginal contribution on top of each technique) is the strongest table in the thesis.
- **Honest, mechanistically analyzed negative results.** The CoT flip analysis (24 recovered vs. 51 broken cases, Fig. 4.2, p. 30) with verbatim reasoning excerpts (Table 4.2, p. 31) explains *why* chain-of-thought hurts, not just that it does. The same goes for the RAG disambiguation analysis (recall@5 of 97.2% yet a 24.5 pp drop, pp. 23–24).
- **The format-alignment experiment is a real contribution.** Two adapters, same data and hyperparameters, training losses 0.203 vs. 0.229, yet 69.75% vs. 76.75% under constrained decoding (Fig. 4.4, p. 26) — this isolates output-format alignment as a training-data design dimension and plausibly extends Gorilla's data-relevance insight, as claimed (p. 40).
- **Statistical self-awareness in places.** The Wilson interval caveat on frontier comparisons (p. 36), the six-run determinism check for CD+Q (p. 15), and the explicit refusal to rank τ-bench conditions within noise (p. 39) show good instincts.
- **Verified internal arithmetic.** I re-checked essentially every delta across Tables 4.5, 4.9, 4.10 and the prose (scaling gaps, quantization penalties, flip counts, τ-bench means); with the exceptions noted below, they are consistent.
- **The scope boundary is honestly drawn.** The thesis says plainly that the cascade router was not built (pp. 2, 38) and that single-call gains do not transfer to multi-turn settings (Section 4.6).

## 4. Evaluation by dimension

**Methodology & rigor.** The cumulative ladder is fit for RQ2 and executed carefully. The main weakness is the baseline construction for RQ1: "Base" is the author's two-stage prompt (`Function name:` / `JSON:`, p. 16) with a brace-matching extraction heuristic, deliberately stripped of Qwen 2.5's native tool-calling chat template. The thesis itself notes Qwen 2.5 was instruction-tuned on tool-use examples (p. 16), yet never reports what the model scores under the official BFCL prompting harness — which is the natural "unoptimized" reference and is far above 1.5%. The 70–78 pp "gap" in the abstract and the RQ1 answer (p. 42) is therefore a property of the evaluation pipeline, not of unoptimized SLMs as a class. The conclusion that the gap is format-compliance, not reasoning, very likely survives this correction, but the magnitude framing does not. Secondary methodological concerns: the few-shot examples were designed by inspecting test-split errors (acknowledged on p. 18 — to the thesis's credit, the leaked information *favors* PE and PE still loses); and the τ-bench user simulator is the same Qwen 2.5 7B model as the agent (p. 16/§3.2.2), where the benchmark's reference results use a frontier-model simulator — a confound the limitations section does not mention.

**Technical & mathematical correctness.** Background math (attention, GQA, LoRA, AWQ) is correct and appropriately scoped. Internal arithmetic is almost entirely consistent. Three concrete defects: (i) Figure 4.3's breakdown (66% / 32% / 2%) is labeled as shares of 209 incorrect predictions, but those percentages are actually shares of all 400 cases (264 + 128 + 8); as shares of 209, the categories sum to 136 and leave 73 incorrect cases unaccounted for. (ii) The statistical treatment stops short: the ±4.1 pp Wilson interval (itself slightly understated; I compute ≈ ±4.3 pp at p ≈ 0.73, n = 400) applies to single proportions, but the headline deltas (e.g., +4.0 pp for CD+FT-aligned over CD, "breaks the no-training ceiling," p. 26) are paired comparisons on the same 400 items and should be tested with McNemar's test — the per-case data clearly exists, since a flip analysis was done for CoT. As written, the thesis's central positive result carries no significance test. (iii) The architectural explanation for parallel = 0% (p. 37) contradicts the multi-call output distributions reported for parallel multiple (p. 33).

**Contribution & novelty.** The contribution is honest application-and-synthesis work, appropriate for an MSc: a systematic combination study that the cited survey [20] identifies as missing. The format-alignment finding and the CD-marginal-contribution table are the most publishable elements. Related work covers the essentials (BFCL, ToolLLM, Gorilla, xLAM, Outlines, τ-bench) but misses the literature closest to its most interesting negative result: work on format restriction degrading reasoning (e.g., Tam et al. 2024, "Let Me Speak Freely?") is directly about the CD-vs-CoT interaction in Section 4.2.5 and should be engaged. Recent grammar-constrained decoding systems beyond Outlines (XGrammar, SGLang) and the APIGen pipeline behind the xlam dataset also belong in Chapter 2.

**Business & practical relevance.** The cascade framing gives the thesis a clear "so what," and the escalation-rate translation (p. 39) is the right way to monetize accuracy points. But two claims outrun the evidence: "production-viable" (RQ3) is answered affirmatively without ever defining a viability criterion — a 27% single-call failure rate with no available structural routing signal (acknowledged on p. 38) is a nontrivial bar to call viable; and "no additional inference cost" for constrained decoding (abstract, p. 36) is asserted without a single latency or throughput measurement anywhere in the thesis. FSM compilation and per-step masking have real, measurable overhead in vLLM; for a deployment-oriented thesis, the absence of any latency table is a gap.

## 6. Questions for the defense

1. Qwen 2.5 ships with a native tool-calling chat template and is evaluated on the official BFCL leaderboard well above 1.5%. Why is your two-stage prompt without that template the right operationalization of "unoptimized," and how does RQ1's answer change if the official harness is the baseline?
2. Section 5.3 says your pipeline is architecturally limited to one call per inference, yet Section 4.8.3 reports responses containing one to four calls on parallel multiple. Which is it, and what exactly differs between the two category setups?
3. Your headline gain — CD+FT-aligned at +4.0 pp over CD — is within the ±4.3 pp single-run confidence interval you yourself quote. You stored per-case predictions; why was no paired test (McNemar) run, and what does it show?
4. The τ-bench user simulator is the same 7B model as the agent. How much of the 4.35% pass rate is agent failure versus simulator failure, and would your "single-call gains do not transfer" conclusion survive a GPT-4-class simulator?
5. You recommend CD+Q as "production-viable" while acknowledging that, post-CD, every failure is silent (structurally valid but semantically wrong) and no routing signal exists. What failure rate, with what detectability, is your viability criterion?
6. You claim constrained decoding comes at "no additional inference cost" but report no latency numbers. What is the measured throughput cost of guided JSON decoding in your vLLM setup?
