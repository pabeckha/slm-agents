## 1. Summary of the thesis

The thesis asks how far inference-time and lightweight training techniques can move a small language model (Qwen 2.5, 0.5B–7B) toward reliable tool calling, framed as widening the local tier of a cascade architecture. It runs a cumulative ablation (constrained decoding, schema enrichment, quantization, few-shot, chain-of-thought, RAG, LoRA) plus an isolation arm without guided decoding, on four BFCL v4 categories and τ-bench retail, with cross-family and GitHub-MCP checks. The three research questions are stated verbatim on p.4: RQ1 the unoptimized SLM↔frontier gap; RQ2 the marginal contribution of each technique applied cumulatively; RQ3 whether an optimized SLM can serve as an upper-bound local tier for single-call cascade tool calling on consumer hardware. The central claim is that constrained decoding converts tool calling from a *format-compliance* problem (fixable structurally) into a *semantic argument-value* problem, and that restoring schema detail dropped by a generic prompt (CD+schema, 89.00% at 7B) recovers most of the residual at zero training cost.

## 2. Overall assessment

This is a strong, unusually self-aware empirical thesis. Its defining quality is methodological honesty: the headline 1.5% baseline is explicitly decomposed into a parser artifact (62.00% under a lenient parser) and an integration artifact (96.00% under the native template), and the same-parser gain from constrained decoding (+10.75 pp) is reported alongside the dramatic strict-parser recovery (+71.25 pp) rather than instead of it. The work carries genuine rigor throughout — paired McNemar tests, Wilson confidence intervals, documented ±0.5 pp run-to-run drift, a dedicated threats-to-validity section, and a claims/evidence/scope table (Table 6.1). The breadth is large for a Master's thesis: a twelve-configuration ladder, a four-size sweep, four contrast families, a real-world MCP probe, and a multi-step agentic benchmark. The central insight — that the dominant residual after constrained decoding is missing *schema information*, not model capacity — is well-supported (CD+schema, +16.25 pp, p < 0.00001) and genuinely interesting.

What holds it back is a cluster of scope and framing issues, most of them pre-disclosed but one of them not. The most dramatic numbers (1.5% → 89%) compare a deliberately crippled baseline against the best configuration, so the "gap closed" is partly self-inflicted — the thesis discloses this, but the abstract and introduction still lead with it. The headline CD+schema result rests on a single model size and a single run. RQ3 is "answered" only as a projection: no cascade router is ever built, so the affirmative answer is close to tautological. And there is one genuine claims-vs-evidence problem the body does not flag: the break-even cost model (§5.4) and the Step-5 fine-tuning recommendation are built on Δp ≈ 0.02 for CD+Q+FT-aligned vs CD+Q — the exact comparison the results section reports at p = 0.38, "within discordant-pair noise."

The contribution is careful measurement and two clean findings (schema-detail restoration; training-format alignment), not a new method — constrained decoding itself is prior work (Willard & Louf / Outlines / XGrammar). That is a solid Master's contribution, appropriately scoped.

**Indicative grade band (advisory, written report only): 10 (B) on the Danish 7-point scale.** Comprehensive fulfilment with a few real but mostly non-fatal, well-bounded weaknesses. To move toward **12**: tighten the three writing-level items below — reframe the 1.5%→89% headline so the integration/parser artifacts are visible at first mention, carry the FT-gain significance caveat into the abstract and deployment guidelines, and soften the break-even analysis to match its own statistics. These are edits, not new experiments. What keeps it off **7** is that the remaining weaknesses (single-family scope, single-size headline, projected cascade) are disclosed scope boundaries, not errors, and the underlying rigor is genuine.

## 3. Strengths

- **Exemplary handling of the baseline artifact.** The parser-sensitivity analysis (strict 1.5% / lenient 62.00% / native-template 96.00%, Table 4.1 and pp.9–10, 19–20) is the kind of disclosure many theses omit. The same-parser CD gain (+10.75 pp) is reported next to the strict recovery (+71.25 pp), p.28.
- **The format-vs-semantic distinction is operationalized, not just asserted.** 394/400 baseline failures shown to be structural (p.9); residual 27.25% characterized by concrete value-convention classes (p.29); CD+schema isolates the information-loss component directly (p.30).
- **Statistical discipline.** Paired McNemar tests on stored per-case files, explicit treatment of the ±0.5 pp run-to-run spread, Wilson CIs (≈±4.1–4.4 pp at n=400, p.38), and honest "borderline" / "directional" language for the FT gains (pp.44, 36).
- **Negative results reported and explained.** Few-shot (−2.5 pp at 7B), CoT (−6.75 pp with a four-way flip analysis, Fig. 4.2 and Table 4.3), and RAG (−24.5 pp, with recall@5 = 97.2% isolating disambiguation as the cause) are all reported and mechanistically diagnosed.
- **The schema-boundary finding is clean.** parallel = 0.0% under the single-extraction design is shown to be structural (by construction), and the joint multi-call schema lifts it to 79.0% at 7B (pp.46–47), demonstrating the limit was the output schema, not capacity.
- **External-validity checks beyond the primary family.** Cross-family CD (Table 4.14), format-aligned degradation across four families (p.36), and the GitHub MCP probe (pp.42–43) give the structural finding support outside Qwen 2.5.
- **Scope is governed explicitly.** Table 6.1 pairs each claim with its evidence and scope; the "frontier range" phrase is repeatedly restricted to BFCL Python Simple AST.
- **Thorough, honest generative-AI disclosure** (Appendix A) and reproducibility details (Appendix B, public repo, pinned software versions, pp.16–17).

## 4. Evaluation by dimension

**Methodology & rigor.** The two-armed design (cumulative ladder + no-guided isolation) is well-matched to RQ2 and is the methodological core of the thesis. Determinism (temperature 0, greedy), the run-to-run audit, and the paired-test protocol are handled carefully. Three weaknesses temper this. (1) The primary evaluation leans on BFCL simple_python, the least demanding category — single-turn, single-candidate, so function selection is trivially 100% and the entire result is about argument extraction; this is acknowledged (§5.3.1) but it narrows the load-bearing evidence considerably. (2) Most of the ladder is single-family (Qwen 2.5); only CD is shown to be family-robust, so the *ordering* of techniques, the PE size-reversal, and the quantization profile are Qwen-only findings (acknowledged, §5.3.1). (3) The cascade — the motivating frame for the whole thesis — is never built; the p ≈ 0.72–0.77 traffic fractions and the cost model are projections assuming an ideal semantic router (§5.4). The PE few-shot examples were authored from test-split errors (no held-out dev split), which the author flags and correctly argues makes PE's failure conservative (§5.3.1).

**Technical & mathematical correctness.** The technical content is sound and well-explained: the FSM/token-mask account of why per-step overhead is low (p.18, with a worked CFG example), the two-stage pipeline (Fig. 3.2), and the single-extraction vs joint multi-call schemas (Fig. 3.4) are all correct and clear. Statistics are appropriate (McNemar for paired binary outcomes; Wilson intervals). The one internal inconsistency is consequential and is logged as Issue 1: the break-even derivation (§5.4) instantiates Δp ≈ 0.02 for CD+Q+FT-aligned vs CD+Q, but §4.4.2 reports that same comparison at p = 0.38, explicitly "directional rather than statistically established." If Δp is statistically ~0, the N ≈ 46,000 break-even is not just imprecise but undefined in direction. The AWQ "interacts more destructively with fine-tuned deltas" claim (2.5 pp vs 0.5 pp) is offered as "a plausible explanation" but the 2.5 pp itself is one comparison near the noise floor — appropriately hedged, but thin.

**Contribution & novelty.** Stated plainly: the contribution is a systematic cumulative ablation of optimization techniques on a single SLM family for tool calling, plus two clean empirical findings — (a) schema-detail restoration recovers most of the post-CD residual without training, and (b) training-output-format alignment, not content, explains a 7.0 pp swing between two otherwise-identical LoRA adapters. This is measurement and synthesis, not a new method; constrained decoding is Willard & Louf / Outlines / XGrammar, and the thesis is honest about this. Related-work coverage is adequate and fairly represented (Gorilla, ToolLLM, xLAM/APIGen, Tam et al., the SLM survey). For a Master's thesis this is a solid, well-positioned contribution; it would be a mistake to dress it as methodological novelty, and the thesis does not.

**Business / practical relevance.** The deployment framing is the applied spine: a five-step decision ladder (§6.2) and a cascade cost model (§5.4) now grounded in sourced list prices (GPT-4o at \$2.50/\$10.00 per M tokens; A100 80GB at ~\$1.50/GPU-h), with a sensitivity case against GPT-4o-mini (break-even > 750,000). This is a real improvement over an illustrative calculation. But the unit economics inherit Issue 1: the break-even rests on a null-ish Δp, and Step 5 recommends fine-tuning without carrying the significance caveat that §4.4.2 is careful to state. The most useful *practical* finding is arguably the one the thesis states against its own headline — for a single frozen model, the native template (96.00%) beats every constrained configuration, so "just use the native template" is Step 1. That intellectual honesty is a strength.

## 6. Questions for the defense

1. Your break-even analysis (§5.4) uses Δp ≈ 0.02 for CD+Q+FT-aligned over CD+Q, but §4.4.2 reports that comparison at p = 0.38, within noise. If that gain is statistically zero, what happens to the N ≈ 46,000 break-even and to your Step-5 recommendation to fine-tune?
2. CD+schema reaches 89% but the native template (B-template) reaches 96% on the same model and hardware with no constrained decoding at all. In what concrete deployment is the constrained pipeline the right choice over simply using the native template — and is "model-agnostic structural guarantee" worth 7 pp in practice?
3. The 16.25 pp CD+schema gain bundles descriptions, enumerations, and defaults. For enum parameters you place the allowed values directly in the prompt. How much of the gain survives if you remove the enum values, and would you still call the rest a "semantic" improvement?
4. Your headline gap (1.5% → 89%) compares a deliberately model-agnostic, strict-parse baseline against your best configuration. How much of RQ1's gap is a property of small models versus a property of the integration you chose to baseline against?
5. CD+schema is reported at 7B and a single run. Given your own ±0.5 pp run-to-run audit, how confident are you that the "enters frontier range" claim is stable, and what would you expect at 0.5B–3B?
6. τ-bench pass rate is 4.35% with a 7B model simulating the user. How much of that floor is the agent and how much is the simulator — and what would a frontier simulator change about your central "single-call gains don't transfer" claim?
