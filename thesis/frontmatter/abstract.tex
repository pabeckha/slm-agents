\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
Large language models have become the default backbone for agentic systems, yet their computational cost and reliance on external APIs limit deployment in latency-sensitive or resource-constrained settings. This thesis investigates whether small language models (1B--8B parameters) can achieve reliable agentic behavior, specifically in reasoning and tool calling, when combined with targeted optimization techniques.

% We evaluate a cumulative pipeline of methods applied to open-weight models such as Qwen 2.5 and Llama 3.2. Starting from an unconstrained baseline, we progressively apply constrained decoding, prompt engineering, inference-time compute strategies (chain-of-thought, ReAct), retrieval-augmented generation, quantization, and LoRA fine-tuning. Each method is assessed individually on the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard (BFCL) and a custom tool-calling benchmark, measuring tool selection accuracy, argument correctness, JSON validity, hallucination rate, and end-to-end task success.

% Results show that constrained decoding alone substantially improves structured output reliability, and that combining no-training techniques narrows the performance gap with frontier models on standard benchmarks. Training-based methods, particularly LoRA fine-tuning on tool-use data, further improve reasoning and tool selection. The findings suggest that the reliability gap between small and large models in agentic tasks stems more from insufficient generation constraints than from model scale, and that a carefully chosen stack of optimization techniques can make small models viable for production-grade agent deployments on consumer hardware.
