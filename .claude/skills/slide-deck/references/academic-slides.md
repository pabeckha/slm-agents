# Academic / MIT Slide Rules

## Differences from McKinsey

Academic slides (thesis defense, conference talk, research review) follow the same
one-claim-per-slide law but differ in tone and evidence standards.

| Rule | McKinsey | Academic |
|------|----------|----------|
| Title | Business assertion | Hypothesis or finding with scope |
| Uncertainty | Rare, footnoted | Explicit — error bars, confidence intervals, p-values |
| Recommendation | Always present | Optional; "future work" replaces it |
| Citations | Rarely on slide | Must appear on the slide that uses the claim |
| Visuals | Clean, branded | Publication-quality; axes in pts, not px |

## Academic Title Patterns

- Finding: "Constrained decoding raises single-call accuracy by 71 pp on BFCL"
- Hypothesis test: "Format alignment, not model size, determines LoRA effectiveness"
- Contrast: "BFCL accuracy does not predict τ-bench pass rate"

Avoid: "Results", "Experiment 3", "Discussion"

## Figure Rules (conference / thesis)

- Every figure needs a caption on the slide (one sentence, below the figure)
- Axes must be labeled with units
- If reproduced from a paper: cite inline "(Smith et al., 2023)"
- Error bars are required for any mean shown across multiple runs

## Citation Placement

- Inline superscript or bracketed number at the end of the claim that uses it
- Full reference in a "References" slide at the end (not counted in slide total)
- Never cite in the title; cite in the body bullet or figure caption

## Thesis Defense Specific

- Slide 1: title, your name, supervisor, date
- Slide 2: one-slide problem statement ("why does this matter?")
- Slide 3: one-slide method overview (even if later slides go deep)
- Second-to-last: limitations and future work (required for academic credibility)
- Last: "Thank you / Questions" — leave key result on screen during Q&A

## Timing Rule

Plan 1.5–2 minutes per content slide for a defended talk. A 20-minute defense = 10–13 content slides maximum.
